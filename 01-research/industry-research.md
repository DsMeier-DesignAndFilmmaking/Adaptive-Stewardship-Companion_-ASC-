# Industry Research

## Purpose

Understand the broader outdoor recreation, hospitality, and conservation industries: trends, regulatory context, funding models, and operational norms that shape what's possible for ASC.

## Key Questions to Answer

- What macro trends affect outdoor organizations' operations and budgets?
- How are conservation/ecological monitoring practices evolving?
- What regulatory or funding constraints shape technology adoption in this space?
- What operational norms differ across guest ranches, parks, land trusts, and eco-lodges?

## Findings

### Scale and trajectory of the outdoor recreation economy

Outdoor recreation generated $1.3 trillion in economic output in 2024 (2.4% of U.S. GDP), but real growth is decelerating — 2.7% in 2024 versus 5.3% in 2023, roughly tracking overall GDP growth rather than outpacing it. Travel, tourism, and local trips make up 48.5% of total value added, tying outdoor recreation's health directly to the hospitality segment ASC also targets. This is a large, mature, slowing-growth industry — not a hyper-growth market a startup can simply ride.

### Visitation is concentrated and straining a small number of sites

The National Park Service recorded 323 million recreation visits in 2025, with new records at multiple parks entering 2026. But roughly half of all visits concentrate at just the top 23 parks, with real congestion effectively limited to 12-15 "destination" parks — Zion topped 4 million visitors in 2025, Yellowstone similarly. This concentration is producing direct, expensive ecological and infrastructure damage: Yosemite had to rebuild Bridalveil Fall's boardwalk and reroute trails after crowd damage, and Yellowstone is spending over $1.5 billion updating wastewater systems strained by visitor volume. Some parks have already moved to visitor caps, timed-entry, and reservation systems as a direct response. This is concrete, sourced evidence that the charter's core problem (visitor experience vs. ecological condition trade-off) is real and costly today — not a hypothetical.

### Public funding for the largest customer category (parks) is under acute stress

The proposed FY2026 federal budget would cut National Park Service operating funds by roughly a third, cut USFS by 22%, and reduce staffing 30% across NPS/USFS/FWS/BLM versus 2024 levels; about 24% of NPS staff departed in just the first half of 2025. Congress partially pushed back, keeping NPS funding roughly flat (~$3.1B) rather than accepting deeper cuts, but the trend and uncertainty are real. This cuts two ways for ASC: agencies have a growing "do more with less" operational need that a decision-support tool addresses directly, but the same agencies have a shrinking, uncertain budget and depleted staff to run a vendor evaluation or procurement process — reinforcing the earlier finding (`customer-segmentation.md`) that public parks are a poor near-term ICP despite being a strong conceptual fit.

### Adaptive management is an established discipline the industry already believes in — and already struggles to execute

"Adaptive management" as a formal concept dates to Holling and Walters/Hilborn's work in the 1970s: a structured, iterative decision process for acting under uncertainty in natural resource management, explicitly not "trial and error." The literature is candid that despite decades of institutional endorsement, adaptive management has been "more influential as a concept than as a process," constrained by political, economic, and cultural barriers to actually running the iterative loop in practice. This matters directly for ASC: the target industry doesn't need to be sold on the idea that decisions should adapt to changing ecological and operational conditions — that's already institutional doctrine. The unsolved problem is operational execution, which is exactly the gap software can fill, but it also means ASC is stepping into a discourse with 50 years of "why didn't this work last time" baggage that discovery interviews should surface directly.

### Frontline staffing is lower-wage, seasonal, and high-turnover — a design constraint, not just a market fact

Within the outdoor recreation economy, higher-wage, year-round jobs concentrate in adjacent information-sector roles (~$143k average), while the hospitality/food-service/accommodation roles that staff guest-facing outdoor operations average around $46k and are disproportionately seasonal. This matters for product design, not just business strategy: ASC's primary named users include naturalists, trail managers, and seasonal staff supervisors — roles likely to sit on the lower-wage, higher-turnover, more seasonal side of this split. Interfaces and onboarding need to assume frequent staff turnover and minimal training time, reinforcing the charter's own principle to reduce cognitive load rather than assuming sophisticated year-round power users.

### Climate change increases the need for adaptive tools precisely as human assessment capacity shrinks

Federal climate-vulnerability assessment capacity for public lands is a direct casualty of the same budget and staffing cuts described above. That's a real tension: climate change increases the rate at which conditions change (raising the value of continuously adaptive, rather than static annual-plan, decision-making), while the same funding pressure reduces the human capacity to do that assessment manually — an argument for software filling the gap, but only where budget exists to pay for it.

### Conservation/nature tech capital is real but concentrated elsewhere

Global "nature tech" venture funding reached $2.1 billion in 2024 (+16% YoY, 258 deals), with a total of $11.4B invested from 2018–2024, and the category is projected to reach $6B by 2030. But 45% of that investment concentrates in Food & Agriculture, and MRV/biodiversity-credit startups are now the leading early-stage sub-category — not visitor-experience-linked operational software. This confirms investor appetite for "nature tech" broadly exists and is growing, but ASC's specific niche (visitor experience + ecological operations, blended) isn't yet where that capital is concentrating — useful context for fundraising positioning, and a sign ASC may need to educate investors on the category as much as customers.

## Validated Knowledge

- Outdoor recreation is a $1.3T, 2.4%-of-GDP U.S. industry with decelerating growth (2.7% real growth in 2024 vs. 5.3% in 2023). *(BEA, Outdoor Recreation Roundtable)*
- Visitor congestion at a concentrated set of "destination" parks is producing measurable, expensive ecological and infrastructure damage today (Yellowstone's $1.5B+ wastewater upgrade, Yosemite's Bridalveil Fall rebuild). *(Outside, DOI, GearJunkie sources)*
- Proposed FY2026 federal budget cuts targeted roughly a third of NPS operating funds and 30% of combined NPS/USFS/FWS/BLM staffing; Congress partially reversed this, holding NPS near flat (~$3.1B). *(Center for American Progress, Forbes, NPCA)*
- Adaptive management is a 50-year-old formal discipline in natural resource management, already institutionally endorsed but self-acknowledged in the literature as chronically under-executed in practice. *(Wikipedia, USFS PNW-GTR-654, PMC)*
- Global nature tech VC funding reached $2.1B in 2024, up 16% YoY, concentrated primarily in food/agriculture and MRV/biodiversity credits. *(Serena Capital VC Funding Trends in Nature Tech 2025)*

## Evidence-Informed Insight

- The "do more with less" pressure on public land agencies is a genuine argument for ASC's value proposition, but it's in direct tension with those same agencies' reduced capacity to run procurement or adopt new vendors near-term — this supports treating public parks as a validated-later, not validated-now, segment (consistent with `customer-segmentation.md`).
- Adaptive management's 50-year history of being "more concept than practice" suggests customer discovery interviews should explicitly ask what's been tried before and why it didn't stick — this is a rich vein of tribal knowledge (past failed tools, past process reforms) that desk research can't surface but interviews can.
- Frontline staff wage/turnover patterns suggest ASC's UX should be validated with seasonal/entry-level staff specifically, not just with the GM/Director-level buyers who'll be the sales contact.

## Hypotheses / Open Questions

- Does climate volatility make adaptive, software-driven decision-making more urgently needed at the specific ICP segments prioritized in `customer-segmentation.md` (botanical gardens/nature centers, eco-lodges/guest ranches) — or is that urgency mostly concentrated in the public-land segment ASC is deliberately deprioritizing?
- Is there a way to position ASC in investor conversations that connects to the growing "nature tech" capital flow (currently concentrated in MRV/biodiversity credits and agriculture) without overclaiming a category fit that doesn't yet exist?
- What specifically has been tried before, inside the target ICP segments, to balance visitor experience against site condition — and why did it stop working or get abandoned? (Only answerable via `01-research/customer-discovery.md` interviews.)
